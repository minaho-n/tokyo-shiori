A note from us

Dear Manasvi,

Three weeks in Japan is a gift you've given yourself, and this day is ours to you. We've tried to trace a path through a Tokyo that moves at its own quiet pace — where mornings belong to coffee and considered things, afternoons to old lanes and patient birds, and evenings to books and the kind of places that stay with you long after you've left.

Go slowly. Follow what interests you. And know that we're thinking of you, happily wandering.

A Kuramae institution where you design and bind your own notebook — choosing cover, paper type, and binding from a carefully curated selection. The result is a one-of-a-kind journal that will outlast the trip.
Allow at least an hour. The custom ink-blending service is popular — check if the booking is required (it seems booking is required on weekends).
Kuramae boutique walk
Tokyo's artisan quarter — a neighbourhood of small-batch makers and considered design. Browse SyuRo for refined tin and leather goods, and Koncent for beautifully designed everyday objects.
Closing days vary shop by shop in Kuramae — many small boutiques are shut one weekday (often Tuesday or Wednesday). Worth checking in advance for any specific shop you want to visit.

Asakusa drift

No agenda needed here. Let the Kaminarimon gate, the incense smoke from Senso-ji, and the Nakamise street wash over you. Loud and alive — a good counterpoint to Kuramae's quiet. Walk through, absorb it, and move on when you're ready.

Yanaka Ginza
谷中銀座
Tokyo's most beloved old shotengai — a covered shopping street untouched by the city's relentless change. Browse, snack, and slow down. The lanes off the main street are just as good.
Shinobazu Pond
不忍池 · 上野恩賜公園
One of Tokyo's finest urban birding spots. Grey herons stand motionless in the shallows. Little egrets drift overhead. Great cormorants dry their wings on wooden posts. Walk the path around the pond and let the city fade out.
Walk slowly around the perimeter — herons and egrets often stand motionless in the shallows. Bring binoculars if you have them.
Yanaka Cemetery
谷中霊園
Far more beautiful than it sounds — mossy paths, old cherry trees, and woodland birds moving through the canopy. Brown-eared bulbuls, Japanese white-eyes, and barn swallows call from the branches.
Nezu Shrine
根津神社
One of Tokyo's most beautiful shrines. A long winding tunnel of vermillion torii gates through forested grounds — far quieter than Kyoto's Fushimi Inari, and all the better for it. The woodland is wonderful for small birds too.

神保町Jimbocho
Tokyo's booktown — old paper, strong coffee, and a very good evening
神
Bookshop browsing
神保町古書街
Over 170 second-hand bookshops along a single street — the world's largest concentration. Even without reading Japanese, the atmosphere is its own reward: stacks of old volumes, vintage maps and prints, specialist collectors. Walk the stretch along Yasukuni-dori before the shops close.
Most bookshops close between 18:00 and 19:00, and many are closed on Sundays. Aim to arrive by mid-to-late afternoon to browse properly.

Keep an eye out anywhere · どこでも
Large-billed Crow
ハシブトガラス
Tokyo's iconic crow — bold, intelligent, everywhere
Lucky finds, if you're patient: Great Cormorant (カワウ) drying its wings on a post, Night Heron (ゴイサギ) roosting in pondside trees, Warbling White-eye (メジロ) flickering through leaves, Barn Swallow (ツバメ) swooping low at dusk, or — rarest of all — the electric-blue flash of a Common Kingfisher (カワセミ) along the water.
If hydrangeas are in bloom
Early June is the very start of Tokyo's hydrangea (紫陽花, ajisai) season — some bushes may already be open, others still budding. If the bloom has started, these spots are worth seeking out. Peak is typically mid-to-late June.
Hakusan Shrine
白山神社
~3,000 hydrangeas; hosts the Bunkyō Ajisai Matsuri in mid-June. ~15 min walk from Nezu Shrine.
Asukayama Park
飛鳥山公園
A famous "hydrangea slope" runs alongside the train tracks — atmospheric and free.
